<h2>My Mum Was an Accountant</h2>

<p>Growing up, I watched her work with numbers the way some people work with language. She could read a P&L, balance a ledger, and translate the story behind the data. But what stuck with me most wasn't the work itself—it was the certainty.</p>

<p>She taught me early: <strong>debits equal credits. Everything must balance.</strong> When you have a balanced ledger, you know you're right. Not "pretty sure." Not "probably." Right.</p>

<p>In a world where most things are grey, accounting is black and white. There are rules. Clear rules. And if you follow them, you can prove you're correct. For a kid who liked order, logic, and being able to confirm his answers, that was everything.</p>

<h2>High School: The Click Moment</h2>

<p>I didn't choose accounting because it looked exciting or because I'd heard it paid well. I chose it because in every assessment, when I finished an accounting assignment—pencil on ledger paper, entries recorded, everything balanced—I had clarity. Certainty.</p>

<p>Other subjects felt theoretical. Subjective. Your English teacher might mark your essay differently than another teacher would. History could be interpreted. But accounting? Either the books balanced or they didn't. Either you understood the rule or you didn't.</p>

<p>That exactness. That logic. That ability to be 100% sure I had the right answer when everything reconciled—that was the click. Not just for a career, but for how my brain works. I'm a person who thrives on order, structure, and being able to prove something works. Accounting gave me that.</p>

<p>So I pursued it.</p>

<h2>University Taught Theory. PwC Taught Speed.</h2>

<p>University was textbooks and frameworks. How to record journal entries. How to prepare financial statements. The mechanics of accounting.</p>

<p>Then came the PwC graduate program. Audit. And everything I thought I knew about accounting got redefined in about three weeks.</p>

<p>In audit, you're time-poor. Severely time-poor. You're under pressure to gather evidence, complete workpaper reviews, investigate discrepancies—all at speed, with accuracy non-negotiable. You can't afford to waste time. You can't afford to make mistakes. Both happen at once or the entire audit falls apart.</p>

<p>That's where I learned what actually mattered: <strong>not just being able to do accounting, but being able to do it fast.</strong></p>

<p>Excel became my language. Not fancy Excel. Efficient Excel. Formulas that worked. Processes that scaled. Shortcuts that saved hours without sacrificing accuracy. My colleagues were grinding through manual processes. I was automating. Thinking ahead about what would break when data changed. Building models that could handle what came next.</p>

<p>Efficiency stopped being a nice-to-have. It became a competitive advantage.</p>

<h2>Flight Centre: Eyes Opened</h2>

<p>After PwC, I landed at Flight Centre as an Operations Accountant. Fresh out of grad year, armed with efficiency habits, ready to go.</p>

<p>Then I looked around.</p>

<p>Smart people everywhere. Graduates who understood financial concepts. People who should have been able to optimize their work. But they were building Excel models that took twice as long as they should. Models with manual steps that broke when data changed. Reconciliations done by hand. Processes that were just—inefficient.</p>

<p>Not because they were incapable. Because they'd never been shown the efficient path.</p>

<p>I started fixing things. Overhauling Excel models. Automating processes. And the impact wasn't just about me saving time—it was about freeing up my colleagues to do actual value-add work instead of grinding through manual data entry and reconciliation.</p>

<p>That was the first hint of something bigger: <strong>efficiency isn't just personal productivity. It's systemic value creation.</strong></p>

<h2>Coronis Changed Everything</h2>

<p>Flight Centre planted the seed. Coronis made it grow.</p>

<p>At Coronis, the CFO specifically brought me in to "revolutionize" our accounting and finance processes. Not just Excel—everything.</p>

<p>And what I found was staggering. Processes that weren't even in Excel. Systems that were archaic. Brilliant people who'd simply accepted "the way it's always been done" because nobody had shown them another option.</p>

<p>Printing invoices. Wet-signing payments. Re-entering data across multiple spreadsheets. Year after year, the same convoluted workflows. Countless wasted hours.</p>

<p>I didn't just fix Excel. I transformed how we worked. Automated printing. Digitized approvals. Eliminated re-entry. Built systems that cut processing time dramatically and freed up the entire team to do real analysis instead of drowning in manual work.</p>

<p>The impact: time saved, money saved, people's stress reduced, and—most importantly—the company could finally see what their actual numbers were telling them.</p>

<p>But here's what I didn't realize yet: <strong>my real value wasn't the efficiency I'd built. It was something else entirely.</strong></p>

<h2>The Mentor Who Changed My Perspective</h2>

<p>A boss pulled me aside one day and said something that shifted everything.</p>

<p>He pointed out that while my efficiency work was valuable, my actual skill—the thing that truly moved the needle for leaders and decision-makers—wasn't the spreadsheets or the speed. It was my ability to take complex financial information and translate it into language people could actually understand and act on.</p>

<p>Processing numbers? Replaceable. Analyzing numbers? Valuable, but still replaceable.</p>

<p>Translating numbers into clarity for the people who make decisions? <strong>That's irreplaceable.</strong></p>

<p>That moment reframed my entire career. Everything I'd done—the efficiency, the optimization, the automation—was just the foundation. The real skill was taking all that and making it matter to the people who needed to understand it.</p>

<h2>The Career Arc I Didn't See Coming</h2>

<p>Looking back, my career followed a path I didn't plan:</p>

<p><strong>Accounting grad</strong> → rule-follower, black and white, everything must balance.</p>

<p><strong>PwC</strong> → efficient processor, speed without sacrificing accuracy.</p>

<p><strong>Flight Centre & Coronis</strong> → systems transformer, freeing people to do real work.</p>

<p><strong>The mentor realization</strong> → translator, making complexity simple for decision-makers.</p>

<p>I chose accounting because I loved the exactness. The rules. The ability to prove you're right.</p>

<p>But my career became valuable because I learned that the real skill isn't following the rules perfectly. It's understanding them so well that you can explain them to people who don't, in a way that moves them to action.</p>

<p>That's what I wish I'd known when I started: the path from rule-follower to translator isn't just about technical skill. It's about understanding that your value compounds when you can bridge the gap between what the numbers say and what people need to hear.</p>
